What Matters Does a Family Lawyer Handle in Karachi?

A family lawyer in Karachi handles all matters falling under the jurisdiction of the Family Courts as defined in the Family Courts Act 1964, Schedule. These courts have exclusive jurisdiction over specific family-related disputes, meaning no other court can hear these matters.

Maintenance Claims (Nafaqa)

Filing and defending maintenance suits for wives, children, and dependent family members. A wife is entitled to maintenance throughout the marriage including food, clothing, housing, and medical expenses. The amount is determined by the husband's financial capacity and the wife's standard of living.

Child Custody (Hizanat)

Filing custody petitions and visitation arrangements before the Family Court. Under Muslim law, mothers have custody rights for boys until age 7 and girls until puberty. Courts prioritize the child's welfare (best interest principle) and can override default rules when necessary.

Dower Recovery (Haq Mehr)

Recovering unpaid dower (haq mehr) as specified in the nikah nama. This includes both prompt dower (muajjal) payable at marriage and deferred dower (muwajjal) payable upon demand or divorce. The Family Court enforces full payment with interest in some cases.

Guardianship Petitions

Filing guardianship applications for minors under the Guardians and Wards Act 1890. This includes appointing guardians for orphaned children, granting guardianship for property management of minors, and custody disputes involving extended family members.

How Does the Family Court Process Work in Karachi?

The Family Court process in Karachi follows a structured procedure designed for faster resolution of domestic disputes. The Family Courts Act 1964 mandates that judges attempt reconciliation before proceeding to trial, and cases should ideally conclude within 6 months.

  1. Filing the suit: Your family lawyer drafts and files the plaint (lawsuit) in the appropriate Family Court, specifying the relief sought (maintenance, custody, dower, etc.).
  2. Pre-trial conciliation: The court appoints a pre-trial mediator or conducts reconciliation proceedings. If the parties reach agreement, a consent decree is issued.
  3. Written statement: The respondent files their defense within 30 days.
  4. Framing of issues: The court identifies the specific questions to be decided during trial.
  5. Evidence stage: Both parties present witnesses, documents, and affidavits. Cross-examination of witnesses occurs at this stage.
  6. Arguments: Both advocates present final arguments based on evidence and applicable law (Muslim Family Laws Ordinance, Sharia principles, precedent).
  7. Judgment: The Family Court issues its decree. Appeals go to the High Court of Sindh under Section 14 of the Family Courts Act within 30 days.

During the proceedings, the court can issue interim orders for maintenance, custody, or protection. These interim orders remain in effect until the final decree. If the husband fails to comply with interim maintenance orders, the court can enforce payment through attachment of property or salary.

How Is Child Custody Decided by Karachi Family Courts?

Child custody in Karachi Family Courts is decided primarily on the welfare-of-the-child principle, supplemented by Islamic law guidelines. The court evaluates the child's physical, emotional, educational, and moral needs before determining custody arrangements.

Islamic law default custody periods (hizanat):

Child Mother's Custody Until After That
Son Age 7 Custody may transfer to father
Daughter Until puberty Courts often extend for welfare reasons

Courts can override these defaults. A mother who remarries may lose custody if the court determines it affects the child's welfare. A father with substance abuse issues may be denied custody regardless of the child's age. The child's preference is considered if they are mature enough to express it. Visitation rights are always granted to the non-custodial parent.

What Is the Wife's Right to Maintenance Under Pakistani Law?

Maintenance (nafaqa) is a wife's enforceable legal right under the Muslim Family Laws Ordinance 1961 and Sharia principles. The husband is legally obligated to provide maintenance throughout the marriage and during the iddat period (waiting period) after divorce. Failure to provide maintenance is grounds for the wife to seek judicial intervention.

Maintenance obligations include:

  • During marriage: Food, clothing, housing, medical treatment, and other necessities proportional to the husband's income and social standing.
  • Iddat maintenance: After divorce, the husband must provide maintenance for the iddat period (3 menstrual cycles or 3 months). This includes housing in the same or equivalent standard.
  • Child maintenance: Fathers are financially responsible for children's food, education, healthcare, and clothing, regardless of which parent has custody.
  • Interim maintenance: During ongoing Family Court proceedings, the court can order the husband to pay interim maintenance to prevent financial hardship.

The Family Court determines maintenance amounts based on: the husband's proven income, the wife's standard of living during the marriage, the number of children, housing and education costs, and inflation. Courts regularly review and increase maintenance amounts.

Can a Woman File for Khula Without Her Husband's Consent?

Yes. Khula is a woman's right to dissolve her marriage without her husband's consent by offering to return her dower (haq mehr). Under Section 10(4) of the Family Courts (Amendment) Act 2015, if the court is satisfied the wife cannot live with her husband within the bounds prescribed by Allah, it passes a khula decree. The husband's consent is not required.

The khula process differs from talaq (husband-initiated divorce) in key ways:

  • Who initiates: Khula is initiated by the wife. Talaq is initiated by the husband.
  • Dower: In khula, the wife typically returns or forfeits her dower. In talaq, the husband must pay all outstanding dower.
  • Court involvement: Khula requires a Family Court decree. Talaq requires notice to the Union Council Chairman and a 90-day reconciliation period.
  • Timeline: Khula cases in Karachi take 3 to 12 months depending on whether the husband contests.

Frequently Asked Questions About Family Law in Karachi

What matters does a Family Court handle in Pakistan?

Family Courts handle: dissolution of marriage (khula/divorce), dower recovery, maintenance claims, custody of children, guardianship petitions, restitution of conjugal rights, jactitation of marriage, and personal property disputes between spouses.

How long does a family suit take in Karachi?

The Family Courts Act mandates 6-month resolution. Practically, contested cases take 1 to 3 years. Khula cases where the wife concedes dower may conclude in 3 to 6 months. Custody disputes with complex evidence requirements take longer.

How is child custody decided in Pakistani family courts?

Custody follows the child's welfare (best interest) principle. Mothers have default custody for boys until age 7 and girls until puberty. Courts can override defaults based on welfare considerations. The child's preference is heard if they are mature enough.

What is the wife's right to maintenance?

Wives are entitled to maintenance (nafaqa) throughout marriage and during iddat after divorce. Maintenance covers food, clothing, housing, and medical expenses. The amount is proportional to the husband's income. Courts enforce maintenance through salary attachment.

Can a woman file for khula without consent?

Yes. A woman can obtain khula without her husband's consent by returning her dower. The Family Court passes a decree if satisfied she cannot live within the marriage. The husband's consent is not required under the 2015 amendment.

What is haq mehr and can it be recovered through court?

Haq mehr (dower) is a mandatory payment from husband to wife specified in the nikah nama. If unpaid, the wife files a recovery suit in Family Court. Courts enforce payment of both prompt dower (muajjal) and deferred dower (muwajjal) with interest.
