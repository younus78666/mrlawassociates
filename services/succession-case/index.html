When someone passes away in Pakistan, their bank accounts are frozen, investments become inaccessible, and debts owed to the deceased cannot be legally collected without a succession certificate. This court-issued document is the key that unlocks a deceased person's financial assets for distribution to legal heirs. Without it, banks, insurance companies, and government departments won't release funds.

What Is a Succession Certificate and Why Do You Need One?

A succession certificate is a legal document issued by the Civil Court under the Succession Act 1925 that authorizes named legal heirs to collect debts owed to and movable property of a deceased person. It serves as official proof of who can legally access the deceased's financial assets.

You need a succession certificate to:

  • Withdraw bank deposits: Banks freeze accounts upon notification of the account holder's death. The succession certificate, listing all legal heirs and their shares, is required to release funds.
  • Claim insurance proceeds: Life insurance companies require a succession certificate before disbursing death benefits to the nominee or legal heirs.
  • Transfer investments: Shares, bonds, mutual funds, and fixed deposits require a succession certificate for transfer to heirs.
  • Collect debts: If the deceased was owed money, heirs need the certificate to legally demand payment from debtors.
  • Transfer vehicle registration: Motor vehicle registration transfer requires proof of succession for the heir to register the vehicle in their name.
  • Pension claims: Government and private pension departments require a succession certificate for family pension disbursement.

How Does the Succession Certificate Process Work in Karachi?

The succession certificate process in Karachi follows a defined procedure before the Civil Court with jurisdiction over the deceased's last place of residence or where the assets are located. The process takes 3 to 6 months for uncontested applications.

  1. Documentation gathering: Your lawyer collects the death certificate, CNIC copies of all heirs, family tree, asset details, and relationship proof.
  2. Petition filing: A succession petition is filed in the Civil Court listing all legal heirs, their relationship to the deceased, and the assets for which the certificate is needed.
  3. Court fee payment: Ad valorem court fees are calculated based on the declared value of the estate (typically 2-3% of asset value).
  4. Newspaper publication: The court orders publication of a notice in at least one national and one local newspaper, inviting objections from anyone claiming heirship. A 30-day objection period follows.
  5. Hearing: After the objection period expires, the court conducts a hearing. If no objections were filed, the process is straightforward. If objections exist, a contested hearing follows.
  6. Court order: The court issues the succession certificate listing each heir and their share. The certificate specifies which assets the heirs can collect.
  7. Asset collection: Heirs present the certificate to banks, insurance companies, and other institutions to collect the deceased's assets.

What Is the Difference Between a Succession Certificate and a Legal Heir Certificate?

These two documents serve different purposes and are issued by different authorities. Understanding which one you need prevents delays in accessing the deceased's assets.

Feature Succession Certificate Legal Heir Certificate
Issuing authority Civil Court NADRA / Union Council
Legal basis Succession Act 1925 NADRA Ordinance / Local Government Act
Purpose Collect debts, bank deposits, investments Identify legal heirs
Bank acceptance Universally accepted for large amounts Some banks accept for small accounts
Timeline 3-6 months 1-4 weeks
Cost Higher (court fees + lawyer) Lower (nominal fees)

For significant estate values (bank balances over Rs. 100,000, property, investments), banks and institutions require the court-issued succession certificate. The NADRA legal heir certificate works for smaller accounts and pension claims but is not universally accepted for large financial transactions.

What Documents Are Required for a Succession Certificate?

A succession certificate application in Karachi requires comprehensive documentation proving the death, the legal heirs, and the assets to be transferred. Missing documents cause delays and repeated court adjournments.

Document Purpose
Death certificate (NADRA / Union Council) Proves the death of the deceased
CNIC copies of all legal heirs Identifies and verifies all heirs
Family tree (shajra nasab) Maps all potential heirs
Birth certificates of heirs Proves parent-child relationship
Marriage certificate (nikah nama) Proves spouse relationship
Bank statements / account details Identifies assets to be transferred
Property documents (if applicable) Identifies immovable property for partition
Will / testament (if any) Shows deceased's wishes for distribution

How Are Inheritance Shares Calculated Under Pakistani Law?

Inheritance shares for Muslim families in Pakistan follow Islamic inheritance principles under the Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act 1962. The calculation considers the deceased's relationships, the presence or absence of specific categories of heirs, and whether the estate includes movable or immovable property.

The succession certificate lawyer ensures accurate share calculations because errors lead to litigation between heirs. For detailed inheritance share breakdowns for Muslim families, see our partition suit page which covers the share table extensively.

Key points about Pakistani inheritance:

  • Fixed shares exist for specific heirs (daughters, widows, mothers, etc.) defined by the Quran and Islamic jurisprudence.
  • Residuary heirs (sons, brothers) receive the remainder after fixed shares are distributed.
  • Will (wasiyyat) limitations: Under Islamic law, a person can bequeath maximum 1/3 of their estate by will. The remaining 2/3 must follow inheritance rules.
  • Non-Muslim families follow the Succession Act 1925, where distribution is equal among children regardless of gender.

Frequently Asked Questions About Succession Certificates in Karachi

What is a succession certificate in Pakistan?

A court-issued document under the Succession Act 1925 authorizing legal heirs to collect bank deposits, investments, debts, and movable property of a deceased person. Banks and institutions require it before releasing the deceased's assets.

How long does it take to get a succession certificate?

Typically 3 to 6 months for uncontested applications, including 30 days for newspaper notice. Contested cases with inheritance disputes take 6 to 18 months. The timeline depends on court schedule and case complexity.

What is the difference between a succession certificate and a legal heir certificate?

Succession certificate: issued by Civil Court for collecting assets. Legal heir certificate: issued by NADRA/Union Council for identifying heirs. Banks accept succession certificates for large amounts. Legal heir certificates work for smaller accounts.

What documents are needed?

Required: death certificate, CNIC copies of all heirs, family tree, relationship proof (birth certificates, nikah nama), bank account details, and any will or testament.

How much does a succession certificate cost?

Costs include: court fees (2-3% of estate value), newspaper publication charges, stamp papers, and advocate fees. Total costs depend on estate value. Contact us at 0336-3120798 for an estimate.

Can a succession certificate be challenged?

Yes. Any person claiming heirship can file an objection during proceedings or challenge the certificate after issuance. Courts can modify or cancel certificates obtained through fraud or suppression of material facts.
